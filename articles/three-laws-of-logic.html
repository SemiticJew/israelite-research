Three Laws of Logic

Semitic Jew • Article

The laws of logic are not man-made inventions. They are reflections of God’s own faithful nature. To know God is to know consistency; to study His creation is to discover order. The three classic laws of logic—identity, non-contradiction, and excluded middle—are everywhere present in Scripture and creation itself.

The Law of Identity: A is A

Things are what they are. God revealed Himself to Moses as “I AM THAT I AM” (Exod 3:14), a statement of identity and constancy. Creation also testifies to this: light is light, earth is earth, man is man. To blur identities is to deny reality. This law protects us from collapsing Israel into “just any people” or Christ into “just any man.” Israel is Israel—descendants of Jacob. Jesus is Jesus—Messiah, Son of David, rooted in Judah’s line.

The Law of Non-Contradiction: A cannot be both A and not A

God is light, and in Him is no darkness at all (1 Jn 1:5). To affirm that something can both be and not be in the same way and at the same time is nonsense. Likewise, it is illogical to claim that the Israelites were simultaneously an African-rooted Semitic people and yet somehow a pale European stock. The biblical, linguistic, and geographic record speaks with one voice: the Israelites were people of color, Black in modern terms1.

The same logic applies to depictions of Jesus. He cannot be both a Judean man of Afro-Asiatic descent and a blond-haired European figure. Scripture grounds Him in the line of David, born in Bethlehem, raised in Galilee, ministering in a land linking Africa and Asia. His appearance must fit His people and His place.

The Law of the Excluded Middle: Either A or not A

Either a proposition is true or its negation is true; there is no middle without collapsing meaning. Jesus is either rooted in Israel’s history or He is not. Israel is either God’s chosen nation or it is not. Applied to identity, the excluded middle guards against comfortable evasions. We cannot say, “Israel could be anyone” or “Jesus could look like anyone.” Truth excludes falsehood.

Logic Embodied in Creation

Genesis 1 is structured logically: domains formed and then filled. The heavens and earth are ordered, and God declares them good in sequence. That same ordered logic applies to humanity’s origin: Adam from the adamah (soil)—earthy, dark, fertile. To paint Adam and his descendants as pale northern figures is to deny the harmony between word, world, and witness. Logic insists on congruence.

Conclusion

The three laws of logic are not abstract games but the fabric of creation and covenant. A is A: Israel is Israel. Not-A is not A: Israel cannot be both Israel and not Israel, Black and not Black. Excluded middle: either the testimony of Scripture is true, or it is false. To confess Scripture is to accept that Israelites were a Black people and that Jesus, son of David, shared in that identity. Truth, reason, and revelation converge.
