Three Laws of Logic

Christ calls His people to love God withall of all of our heart, soul, mind, and strength (Mark 12:30). So, when we approach the Bible, we must approach the sacred text with sound thinking. Keeping this in mind, we need not see sound thinking as a rival to faith; it is a servant of faithful reading. When I approaching the text for everyday Bible study, there are three basic rules—often called “the laws of logic”—keeping our thoughts clear and our claims consistent with Scripture: the law of identity, the law of non-contradiction, and the law of excluded middle.

1) The Law of Identity

This law states: A is A. A thing is what it is. When Scripture names or defines something, we should keep that identity steady across our interpretation. If Paul speaks of the gospel as “the power of God unto salvation” (Romans 1:16), we may not quietly redefine “gospel” to mean something different than what the text means about the gospel in its full context. The Law of Identity guards meaning.

  • Names and titles: When Jesus is called “the Lamb of God” (John 1:29), that title has a definite meaning drawn from Scripture concerning the sacrificial laws of God, not from our imagination.
  • Contexts and covenants: “Israel,” “congregation,” “law,” “grace”—each term carries a biblical identity that must be honored in its original context (e.g., Jeremiah 31:31).

2) The Law of Non-Contradiction

This law states: A cannot be both non-A and A at the same time and in the same sense. Scripture cannot teach mutually exclusive claims at all, Plainly stated, God “cannot deny himself” (2 Timothy 2:13). If our understanding of one passage forces a contradiction with another, either we have changed the time, the sense, or we have misunderstood.

  • Time and sense: “Justified” by faith (Romans 5:1) and “justified” in another sense (shown genuine) in James 2:24 are not contradictions; they use the same word in different senses.
  • Harmonizing with humility: We should let clear texts illumine the less clear (Psalm 119:130).

3) The Law of Excluded Middle

Either A or not-A. For a definite claim taken in a definite sense, there is no third option. For example, Elijah pressed our forefathers in the nation of Israel: “If the YHWH be God, follow him” (1 Kings 18:21). Jesus said, “No man can serve two masters” (Matthew 6:24). When the Bible sets a true alternative, we should not blur it into a comfortable middle. Ever, but people do this all the time.

  • True alternatives: Life or death (Deuteronomy 30:19); light or darkness (1 John 1:5–7).
  • Not a denial of nuance: Many issues are complex, but when God gives a command or a promise, obedience cannot live in an undefined middle. Either we are hot or cold, there's no in between

How the Laws Serve Bible Study

These simple tools protect us from sloppy reading:

  • They stabilize meaning (identity).
  • They expose misreadings that collide with other texts (non-contradiction).
  • They clarify commitments when God sets a real choice (excluded middle).

Paul urged the church to “hold fast the form of sound words” (2 Timothy 1:13) and to “prove all things” (1 Thessalonians 5:21). The laws of logic help us do both things in love.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Equivocation: Quietly changing a word’s meaning mid-argument (violates identity).
  • Both-and where Scripture says either-or: Refusing the choice God gives (violates excluded middle).
  • Forcing a contradiction: Ignoring time/sense or wider context (violates non-contradiction).

Practice: A Simple Method

  1. Define terms: What do the key words mean in this passage? Trace cross-references.
  2. Check harmony: Does my reading fit the rest of Scripture on this theme?
  3. Receive the alternative: If God sets two ways, which one am I choosing?
Logic does not add to Scripture; it helps us trace the lines God already drew.

Conclusion

The Bible invites disciplined, and reverent thinking. By honoring identity, refusing contradiction, and accepting God-given alternatives, we seek clarity that serves God and man in love. “Think over what I say,” Paul wrote, “for the Lord will give you understanding in all things” (2 Timothy 2:7). May our minds be steady and our worship sincere.
