Gentiles

Semitic Jew • Article

Language drifts over time. Words pick up new meanings and shed old ones. That’s why it’s dangerous to take an everyday word today and force it to mean what Jesus or David meant then1. The English term Gentile comes to us through Latin (gentilis) and often hides what the Hebrew and Greek actually say in Scripture.

What does “Gentile” mean in Scripture?

Across the canon the underlying words point to four overlapping ideas:

1) “Beget” — kin by descent (the gen-root)

In Greek, the family root genos (“kind, stock, race, family”). Peter calls believers a “chosen generation” (1 Pet 2:9), and Mark notes the Syrophoenician woman was “by nation/ethnicity Syrophoenician” (Mk 7:26)2.

2) “Family / Ethnicity” — a people as a house or clan

Jacob is promised “a nation and a company of nations” (Gen 35:11), and Israel is called God’s “holy nation” (Ex 19:6)3. In the NT the same appears with ethnos and genos, including Israel (cf. Lk 7:5; Jn 11:51–52).

3) “Nation / People” — political or cultural bodies

The Tanakh uses goy/goyim broadly—sometimes of other nations, sometimes of Israel (Deut 4:6–7; Ezek 2:3). Likewise, the NT’s ethnos can mean Israel or other nations (Jn 11:51)4.

4) “Non-Jew” — outside Judea’s polity

Second Temple usage: “Galilee of the Gentiles” (Isa 9:1–2; Mt 4:15). Diaspora Israelites: “Will he go to the dispersed among the Gentiles, and teach the Gentiles?” (Jn 7:35)5.

Israelites called “Gentiles”

Israel as “nation(s)”: same goy/goyim. Ethnos for Israel: Caiaphas: “that nation” (Jn 11:51). Diaspora Jews: “dispersed among the Gentiles” (Acts 2:5–11). Apocrypha: Jews under Hellenism vs. Gentiles (1 Macc 1:11; 1 Macc 3:48)6.

Beware the Anachronous Fallacy

Loading “Gentile” with modern sense (“non-Jew”) overrides the authors’ categories7.

Biblical harmony

Promise to Abraham: “in thee shall all families/nations be blessed” (Gen 12:3; 18:18). Servant: “a light to the Gentiles” (Isa 49:6). NT: ingathering of nations without erasing Israel (Rom 11:17–24; Eph 2:11–19)8.

Conclusion

As you can see, the term Gentile has contextual meaning. The evidence is clear Israelites can be called gentile depending upon the context. It's important we read the Bible in its original context, and avoid pitfalls created by modern theologians. For example, many Christians assume “to the Jew first, then to the Gentile” means “ethnic Jews or those practicing Judaism, and every other ethnicity outside of that context” — but this is contextually unbiblical and historically inaccurate.
