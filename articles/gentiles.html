Who Are the “Gentiles”?

Author

One of the most important endeavors when approaching the Scripture is understand the Bible in its cultural context, and understanding how they used certain everyday words. In most English Bibles, the word “Gentile(s)” is mostly understood by the reader to mean “non-Israelite.” But the underlying biblical word is much broader. The Hebrew goy/goyim and the Greek ethnos/ethnē mean "heathen," “nation(s),” “peoples,” or “outsiders” depending on the context. There are occasions when the “nation” in view is Israel; other times it’s the nations surrounding the Israelites. It's imperative we read wisely and allow the passage to interpret itself which provides a better understanding of the Bible.

The Words & Context Behind “Gentiles”

Hebrew: goy. God calls Israel a “holy nation” (Exodus 19:6). God promised Abraham he would become a “great nation” (Genesis 12:2). The same word goy can also refer to surrounding peoples (Psalm 2:1).

Greek: ethnos. In the New Testament, ethnos can mean someone from the Israelite “nation” (e.g., “he loves our nation,” Luke 7:5; also John 11:51–52) or the other nations (Matthew 28:19).

When “Gentiles” Means Israel

  • Israel is a nation: “a holy nation” (Exodus 19:6); “great nation” promised to Abraham (Genesis 12:2).
  • Greek usage for the Jews as a nation: “our nation” (Luke 7:5); Jesus would die “for that nation” (John 11:51–52).

These examples show the words translated “Gentile(s)” elsewhere can, in different contexts, point directly to Israel being understood as a nation among nations.

When “Gentiles” Means the Other Nations

The context does contrasts Israel with surrounding peoples, frequently, which could be the reason most people are confused about what definition is used contextually. For example, Israel is warned never to imitate the practices of surrounding nations (Leviticus 18:3). In the New Testament, the apostles told to go into “all nations” (Matthew 28:19). Here, some believe the “nations” in this context are referring to non-Israelite peoples. However, not understanding God scattered all Israel and would scatter all of what remained of Judah/Benjamin in 70 AD.

How Do We Decide? Three Simple Checks

  1. Immediate context: Is the passage contrasting Israel with others, or simply referring to a people group (which could be Israel)?
  2. Verb/description cues: Phrases like “our nation” or promises to Abraham often indicate Israel; warnings about “the nations” usually point outward.
  3. Broader storyline: How does the verse fit covenant themes (Abrahamic promise, Sinai, scattering/returning, the nations streaming to Israel)?

Why It Matters

Precision protects meaning. If we flatten “Gentiles” to always mean “non-Israelites,” we will miss places where Scripture is speaking of Israel as a nation or a scattered nation or where the focus is the inclusion of other nations in God’s plan. Letting the text lead us keeps our theology anchored to God’s words rather than our assumptions or common understands that are held widely as truth.

“Gentiles” is an English convenience. The Bible’s own terms (goy, ethnos) carry a range that must be read in context.

Conclusion

Does the term “Gentiles” mean Israelites or non-Israelites? The honest biblical answer is: It's both, but depends on the context. The same words can point to Israel as a nation or to the surrounding nations. To all careful readers, we must pay close attention to how each passage uses the term, by honoring the God who speaks clearly and expects His people to “prove all things; and hold fast that which is good” (1 Thessalonians 5:21).

Notes: On Hebrew goy and Greek ethnos, see standard lexicons; observe usage where Israel is called a “nation” (Exod 19:6; Gen 12:2) and where “nations” clearly means non-Israelites (Matt 28:19).
