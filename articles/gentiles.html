Who Are the “Gentiles”?

In many English Bibles, “Gentile(s)” often sounds like a technical word for “non-Israelite.” But the underlying biblical words are broader. The Hebrew goy/goyim and the Greek ethnos/ethnē simply mean “nation(s),” “peoples,” or “outsiders” depending on the context. Sometimes the “nation” in view is Israel; other times it’s the surrounding peoples. Reading wisely means letting the passage itself tell us which sense is intended.

The Words Behind “Gentiles”

Hebrew: goy. God calls Israel a “holy nation” (Exodus 19:6). Abraham is promised to become a “great nation” (Genesis 12:2). The same word goy can also refer to surrounding peoples (Psalm 2:1).

Greek: ethnos. In the New Testament, ethnos can mean the Jewish “nation” (e.g., “he loves our nation,” Luke 7:5; also John 11:51–52) or the other nations (Matthew 28:19).

When “Gentiles” Means Israel

  • Israel is a nation: “a holy nation” (Exodus 19:6); “great nation” promised to Abraham (Genesis 12:2).
  • Greek usage for the Jewish nation: “our nation” (Luke 7:5); Jesus would die “for that nation” (John 11:51–52).

These examples show the words translated “Gentile(s)” elsewhere can, in other contexts, point directly to Israel understood as a nation among nations.

When “Gentiles” Means the Other Nations

Frequently, the context clearly contrasts Israel with the surrounding peoples. For example, Israel is warned not to imitate the practices of the nations (Leviticus 18:3), and the apostles are sent to “all nations” (Matthew 28:19). Here, “nations” are non-Israelite peoples.

How Do We Decide? Three Simple Checks

  1. Immediate context: Is the passage contrasting Israel with others, or simply referring to a people group (which could be Israel)?
  2. Verb/description cues: Phrases like “our nation” or promises to Abraham often indicate Israel; warnings about “the nations” usually point outward.
  3. Broader storyline: How does the verse fit covenant themes (Abrahamic promise, Sinai, exile/return, the nations streaming to Zion)?

Why It Matters

Precision protects meaning. If we flatten “Gentiles” to always mean “non-Israelites,” we may miss places where Scripture is speaking of Israel as a nation or where the focus is the inclusion of other nations in God’s saving plan. Letting the text lead us keeps our theology anchored to God’s words rather than our assumptions.

“Gentiles” is an English convenience. The Bible’s own terms (goy, ethnos) carry a range that must be read in context.

Conclusion

Does “Gentiles” mean Israelites or non-Israelites? The honest biblical answer is: It depends on the context. The same words can point to Israel as a nation or to the surrounding nations. Careful readers attend to how each passage uses the term, honoring the God who speaks clearly and expects His people to “prove all things; hold fast that which is good” (1 Thessalonians 5:21).

Notes: On Hebrew goy and Greek ethnos, see standard lexicons; observe usage where Israel is called a “nation” (Exod 19:6; Gen 12:2) and where “nations” clearly means non-Israelites (Matt 28:19).
